How Much Caffeine Is Too Much? Science-Backed Daily Limits

March 2026 7 min read by Caffiend

The short answer: 400mg per day for healthy adults, according to the FDA. But that number assumes you weigh roughly 70–80kg, aren't pregnant, and aren't taking any medications that affect caffeine metabolism. For a lot of people — smaller adults, teenagers, pregnant women, anyone sensitive to stimulants — 400mg is genuinely too much. Understanding where your personal limit actually sits requires a bit more than a single headline figure.

The official guidelines

Regulatory bodies and health organisations have converged on broadly similar upper limits for daily caffeine intake:

  • Healthy adults: up to 400mg/day (FDA, EFSA, Health Canada)
  • Pregnant women: no more than 200mg/day (WHO, NHS, American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists)
  • Adolescents (12–18): no more than 100mg/day (American Academy of Pediatrics)
  • Children under 12: no caffeine recommended
  • People with cardiovascular conditions: consult a doctor — no universal limit

For pregnant women, the 200mg limit exists because caffeine crosses the placental barrier freely, and the developing fetus lacks the liver enzymes to metabolise it. Chronic high intake is associated with lower birth weight and, at very high doses, increased miscarriage risk.

Put it in context: A standard filter coffee contains roughly 80–120mg of caffeine. A double espresso runs around 120–140mg. A 250ml energy drink is typically 80mg. The 400mg daily limit is roughly 3–4 standard coffees — less than many people actually consume.

Why body weight changes everything: the mg/kg approach

A flat "400mg" limit is a blunt instrument. Pharmacologists don't typically think in flat milligrams — they think in milligrams per kilogram of body weight (mg/kg). Caffeine distributes through body water, so a 60kg person and a 90kg person processing the same 400mg dose are having very different experiences.

The research-backed effective range for cognitive and physical performance benefits is 3–6mg/kg. Effects are reliable from around 3mg/kg; diminishing returns (and increasing side effects) kick in above 6mg/kg. Most tolerance and safety research also uses mg/kg as the baseline unit.

Converting the FDA's 400mg limit into mg/kg reveals that it's most conservative for lighter people: 400mg ÷ 60kg = 6.7mg/kg, which is already above the recommended performance range. For a 50kg person, 400mg equates to 8mg/kg — a dose likely to cause significant anxiety and jitteriness in anyone who isn't highly tolerant.

The practical takeaway: if you weigh under 65kg, the FDA's 400mg/day is not necessarily safe for you — it may push you past 6mg/kg, where side effects are common. A more personalised limit is closer to 5–6mg/kg of your actual body weight.

How many drinks does 400mg actually look like?

Knowing your limit in milligrams is useful. Knowing what that translates to in real drinks is more useful:

  • Filter coffee (240ml mug): ~95–120mg → 400mg = roughly 3.5 mugs
  • Double espresso: ~120–140mg → 400mg = roughly 3 double shots
  • Flat white / latte (single shot): ~60–75mg → 400mg = roughly 5–6 drinks
  • Red Bull (250ml): 80mg → 400mg = 5 cans
  • Monster Energy (500ml): 160mg → 400mg = 2.5 cans
  • Pre-workout supplement: 150–300mg per serving → 400mg = 1–2 scoops maximum
  • Cola (330ml can): ~35mg → 400mg = over 11 cans

Note that these figures assume only one source of caffeine. Most people stack sources throughout the day — morning coffee, mid-morning latte, afternoon energy drink — and the total accumulates quickly. Chocolate, tea, and some medications (certain headache tablets contain 65–100mg of caffeine) also add to the total.

Symptoms of too much caffeine

Exceeding your personal limit — whether that's 250mg or 500mg — typically produces a recognisable cluster of symptoms. These aren't dangerous in isolation but are a clear signal to cut back:

  • Anxiety and restlessness — caffeine blocks adenosine receptors and increases adrenaline, amplifying the stress response
  • Heart palpitations — a racing or irregular heartbeat, especially noticeable at rest
  • Jitteriness and tremor — fine hand tremor or a general feeling of being unable to sit still
  • Insomnia — difficulty falling asleep or staying asleep, even hours after your last drink
  • Headache — paradoxically, both too much and too little caffeine can trigger headaches
  • Gastrointestinal discomfort — caffeine stimulates gastric acid production and increases gut motility
  • Increased urination — caffeine is a mild diuretic at higher doses
  • Muscle twitching — particularly in the eyelid (a common complaint at moderate excess)

If you regularly experience two or more of these, your daily intake is likely above your personal threshold — even if it's technically within the "safe" 400mg guideline.

Caffeine overdose: when it becomes dangerous

True caffeine toxicity is rare from food and drink sources alone, but it does occur — primarily from highly concentrated caffeine supplements and pure caffeine powder. The FDA has linked multiple deaths to pure caffeine powder, which is why it banned bulk retail sale in the US.

A caffeine overdose typically requires doses above 1,000mg (1g) in a short period, though sensitive individuals — particularly those with underlying heart conditions — can experience serious effects at lower doses.

Warning signs of caffeine overdose (seek medical attention): severe nausea and vomiting, rapid or irregular heartbeat (tachycardia or arrhythmia), uncontrollable shaking, high blood pressure, confusion or disorientation, and in severe cases, seizures or cardiac events. If you or someone else experiences these symptoms after high caffeine intake, call emergency services.

The lethal dose of caffeine in adults is estimated at around 10g — an amount essentially impossible to consume through drinks (you'd need over 80 standard coffees in rapid succession). The practical danger comes from concentrated supplements, caffeine tablets, or energy shots taken in quantity.

High-risk groups

Even at doses well below overdose thresholds, certain groups face elevated risk from caffeine consumption:

  • People with heart arrhythmias or hypertension
  • Those taking certain antidepressants (MAOIs, some SSRIs) or stimulant medications
  • People with anxiety disorders — caffeine reliably worsens anxiety symptoms
  • Individuals with gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD)
  • Anyone with a history of caffeine-induced panic attacks

Tolerance and the moving target problem

Regular caffeine consumption leads to tolerance through upregulation of adenosine receptors. This means your subjective experience of a given dose diminishes over time — but the cardiovascular and metabolic effects don't disappear to the same degree.

Heavy daily drinkers often feel "normal" at doses that would give a casual consumer severe palpitations. Tolerance to caffeine's sleep-disrupting effects is also incomplete: even habitual drinkers who report no sleep issues show measurable reductions in slow-wave (deep) sleep when caffeine levels are elevated at bedtime. The perceptual signal disappears before the physiological impact does.

This is a meaningful reason to track actual milligrams rather than relying on how you feel. Your subjective experience of caffeine is not a reliable guide to whether you're within a healthy range.
